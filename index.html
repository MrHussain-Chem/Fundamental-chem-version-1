How to revise — and why it works

You might already be putting time into revision — but if it isn’t showing up in your results, the way you’re revising might need a tweak. Here’s the approach that works.

Step 1 — Recap a little, often (20–30 mins a week)

What to do: Each week, spend a short block recapping chemistry — and aim for roughly the same amount of recap time as the lessons you had that week. If you have chemistry twice in a week, aim for about an hour of recap at home across the week.
What to focus on: Look back at your assessments, mock exams and homework booklets and note every area you’re weak in. For most people that’s Year 9 content — and we want to go into Year 11 on solid foundations, with Year 9 and 10 already revised and practised. Also: if you couldn’t answer the starter questions in class, those are your weak areas too — start there.
Getting better once isn’t enough. Being good at a topic today doesn’t mean you’ll still be good at it in six months. Revisit content throughout the year. Repetition and practice are what make it stick.

How do I actually recap?

Flashcards for definitions, equations, unit conversions, symbols, and any small question you keep making silly mistakes on. Use them daily until you get the answer word-for-word right 3–4 days in a row, then revisit a week later.
Summary sheet / mind map for broader content: summarise a topic onto a double-sided sheet. If it’s good, it should help you answer the homework booklet. If it doesn’t, fix it using the correct language from the mark scheme. Learn it by reading and writing it out loud 3 times, then writing it from memory 3 times.

Step 2 — Test whether it’s working (10–15 mins a week)

What to do: Spend a short block doing about 15 marks of exam questions on the topics you’ve been recapping. In the two weeks before a test, build this up to around an hour a week. You’ll find questions in your homework booklets and previous in-class assessments.
Still making mistakes? First check your revision resources aren’t wrong. If they’re fine and you’re still slipping up, it’s probably a gap in understanding — bring those exact questions to a lesson or intervention session, or reach out to your chemistry teacher for help and support. We can’t rely on surface-level chemistry for top grades, and the more you practise, the more your confidence grows.

In lessons: focus, ask for help when something doesn’t make sense, answer questions even if you might be wrong, and bring your equipment. Mistakes along the way are completely fine.

Step 2 of 7

Learn the essentials

Read one box at a time. Don’t rush — these are the facts everything else builds on.

The atom. Every substance is made of atoms. An atom has a tiny central nucleus (containing protons and neutrons), surrounded by electrons in shells (energy levels).

The three particles

ParticleChargeRelative massWhere
Proton+11Nucleus
Neutron01Nucleus
Electron−1Almost 0Shells
Atomic number = number of protons (the bottom number).
Mass number = protons + neutrons (the top number).
Neutrons = mass number − atomic number.
Electronic structure. Electrons fill the inner shells first. Shell 1 holds up to 2, shell 2 holds up to 8, shell 3 holds up to 8. Example: sodium (11 electrons) = 2, 8, 1.
Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same number of protons) with a different number of neutrons.
The periodic table. Groups are the columns — same number of outer electrons, similar properties. Periods are the rows. Elements are arranged by atomic number.
Group 1 (alkali metals): 1 outer electron, reactivity increases down the group.
Group 7 (halogens): 7 outer electrons, reactivity decreases down the group.
Group 0 (noble gases): full outer shell, so very unreactive.

🧠 Think first: protons = atomic number. So how many protons?
📋 Next clue: neutrons = mass number − atomic number = 19 − 9.
✅ Last step: a neutral atom has the same number of electrons as protons.

2. Define the term “isotope”, using an example. [2 marks]

Show what a strong answer includes

✓ Atoms of the same element with the same number of protons.

✓ But a different number of neutrons.

Example: Carbon-12 and Carbon-14 both have 6 protons, but 6 and 8 neutrons. Spelling isn’t marked — it’s the chemistry that counts.

3. Explain why noble gases (Group 0) are unreactive. [2 marks]

Show what a strong answer includes

✓ They have a full outer shell (of electrons).

✓ So they do not need to gain, lose or share electrons.

Examiner tip: In “explain” questions, you must give a reason — not just a fact. “Full outer shell” earns one mark; saying why that makes them unreactive earns the second.

The periodic table arranges elements in a particular way. Describe how elements are arranged, and explain why elements in the same group have similar chemical properties. [4 marks]

Reveal model answer & what’s missing

✓ Elements are arranged in order of increasing atomic number.

✓ Elements in the same group are in the same column.

✓ They have the same number of electrons in their outer shell.

✓ The number of outer electrons decides chemical properties, so same outer electrons = similar reactions.

Check your answer: Did you mention atomic number AND outer electrons? Those are the two ideas the examiner is looking for. If you got the gist but missed a word, that’s fine — add it now in another colour.
